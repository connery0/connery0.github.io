The fog is coming

A 48 hour gamejam project with the help of 1 musician

After the chaotic fun of the first jam I returned the year after, but with a new team, partnering up with Rambunctious Flan to provide the music.
And being very grateful for the wonderfull assets provided by Kenny.nl


With the theme of “Roles Reversed”, it was a challenge to figure out a concept we were both excited for, until we settled on, “Reverse 4x strategy”. Where instead of playing as the expansionist player cities, you control the Fog of war, trying to creep back over the map that was once yours.

Because this game required some AI to convey the idea of opposing a 4X player/kingdom, it did not seem feasable to make it a static puzzle (like the previous jam’s entry), so instead this game started relying on random map generation, with predictable enemy behaviours.
Unfortunately, the concept very agressively tries to feature creep itself in such a short deadline, but we managed to contain it to 3 unique enemy types and 8 difficulty levels that have their own challenges that the random maps will throw at you.

The final over results rank our game 481 out of 6752 submissions. Making the game part of the 7% of best recieved games in the jam!
While the #206 spot on creativity even lands us in the top 3%!

Dice Per Mutation

A 48 hour gamejam project with the help of 1 designer

My first experience joining a game jam, after teamming up with talented artist and designer Lynte, we managed to produce a game I’m really proud of from idea to submission in less then 48 hours.


After a few hours of brainstorming we came up with a fun idea, Chess but using the RTS concept of ECON to buy new units based on the movement of pieces and dice.
Halfway troughout the first day we scrapped this idea for a much better one however, a style of game mostly associated with Zachtronics.
A puzzle game about rolling, deconstructing and reconstructing dice on a limited field with increasingly unique opperations.

While exciting, it also brought a lot of issues to figure out on the spot, while there are a lot of ways to fake a die roll to get a random number or side, there was no time to find a system that cared as deeply about a die face, its neighbours and rotations as we needed for the project.
So the only option that remained was improvising one on the spot. This easily cost the majority of our remaining development time. But fortunately a lot of the puzzles and balancing could be done by Lynte on paper while this was progressing. Which then got quickly imported into the game to bring it all together.
The final results rank our game 1570 out of 6050 submissions. Making the game part of the 25% of best recieved games in the jam.

Ordinary heroes

Rapid prototyping and a hunt for the right kind of fun.

A new blend of an action game mixed with 2d towerdefense. An idea that has easily passed 10 different prototypes in rapid succession, and required a very flexible framework to try out new ideas, to find what works with the vision.

A top down design experience, aproached by Cow Studios with a story and game concept, this game went trough a large number of core mechanics to find its identity on a gameplay front that empowers the desired story.
A game framework built around modularity and hot swapable pieces by neccesity, as the design and gameplay has changed a lot during development multiple systems had to be reworked rapidly while carrying over as much functionctionality as possible for the next batch of itterations. This project had custom data editors only showing the relevant data on each game object for designers, collapsable and swappable values and behaviours depending on the desired play style, simply by clicking the right toggles. It also loads in gameplay data from online spreadsheets to enable designers to tweak the content of the game from a single given build. Once the idea of an upgrade tree was finalized, we needed a system to tweak the power and unlock requirements for every power, to give more power to the creative team and allow the programmers to focus on development, I created a flexible unlock editor that allows changes to these parameters without touching a line of code.

Rise of Humanity

Constructing, Updating and Using an AI desciscion tree editor to bring new enemy types to life.

A card game of humans vs a robot wielding super ai, Ironically needed a lot of AI work to make them use their cards in a clever way.

Because every robot in this game, comes with its own deck, they have to think about what cards they can afford to play, and convincingly deal with a good and bad hand from the players point of view.
This quickly led to idea of an descision tree, based on each mechs needs/personality.
Together with Robin Cordemans we created and throughout development updated a custom editor inside of unity, to allow enemies to analize the cards in their hand and make descisions based on the board state.
For this purprose we created a node graph, to be able to quickly get an overview of its logic, and to allow for very rapid prototyping and adjustments of a units behaviour.
My main work involved the “executioner” MARV, and the “support” DRONE.

The Marv generally was always on the lookout for any low health opponents to take out, but its awkward range on attacks means it has to move around a lot to get his desired targets in attacking range for all his options.
Meanwhile the cowardly drone has to avoid opponents that could destroy it, but get close enough to harmed allies to heal them up, and when at low health it enters a desperation state where charges at its opponents with one final surprise.

Wijnegem App

Multi floor indoor navigation, Ar gameplay, promotion coupons and api

What started as a simple promotional app to win coupons in AR grew quickly into a personalized google maps app in a location with incredibly limited gps reception

This project was commisioned by Wijnegem Shopping center
I was half of the 2 programmers on this team, and worked on the majority of this app
This project presented multiple challenges

  • The initial pitch was an AR game.
    • The app had to be able to hide presents or logos around the user, at predetermined spots on the map.
  • Dynamically load in challenges and coupons for the player, with a rapidly changing API backend.
  • Ios and Android feature parrity.
  • Indoor navigation.
    • Because of the scale of the shopping centre, convential GPS location was not accurate enough to guide users.
    • Required multiple bluetooth bluetooth beacons as anchorpoints to orient the app with.
  • AR navigation and pathfinding.
    • Heavy reliance on devices internal gyroscopes and bluetooth anchor points resulted in smooth navigation along a manually created navmesh of the area

Incoming, VR ship simulation

Exploring the use of VR for a ship bridge

A project made based on a request to experiment around the uses of VR/AR on a ship bridge in the near future.
The complete project was planned, pitched, created and presented in 3 months.


This is a project made with a team of 2 artists and 3 gameplay developers.
(Emile Van Den Berghe, Timothy Hsu, Nikos Van den Broek, Andreas Meletiadis-Megas, Tom Partoens)


My job? Most of my work on this project was focussed on two aspects, the tutorial and detection gameplay.
Tutorial This project was aimed at people not familiar with VR, so an explanation had to be added to make the player familiar with the controls in a safe environment, with clear instructions, without underestimating possible prior knowledge.
This was done by making all help messages context sensitive to how much of the controls the players were using.
Detection gameplay When on the actual bridge you have to manage two key parts of the game, managing disasters, and handling unknown ships on the map.
When an unknown ship appears the player gets the ability to request information, and open a (limited) dialogue to try and identify their affiliation. After this process the player decides if they can let them pass or if they want to open fire.


This entire project was created in Blueprints in Unreal Engine 4, and works on all currently available headsets.

AI Survival

Survive a sandbox with minimal information

The challengeÔºö We were given a pre-made engine, and had to make an plugin to act as an AI to survive in the given randomly generated world.
The only information available to the bot is it’s own inventory, a changing waypoint and entities in it’s vision.
Death is caused by not killing enough “zombies”, running out of health or running out of energy, which drains your health.

The above video will showcase the most important parts of my bots AI.

The bot survives off a dynamic interest system.
It is constantly putting a value on how interesting it thinks multiple goals are.
For example, the interest in heading towards the checkpoint is always positive because it provides 10 points, but it will be seen as a lot more interesting if the AI is already close, or if it’s in an unvisited house.
The interest in exploring the map on the other hand depends on how well prepared the bot is. If the bot has filled up it’s inventory with the necessary items for survival (weapons and restorative items) it will try to continue mapping out the playing field, to increase it’s knowledge of all available houses.

City AI Simulation

How to simulate large crowds and make them look natural

A project made in 2 months, the goal was to find a way to simulate a bustling city, one with a large number of inhabitants following their own agendas, while avoiding unnatural behaviour.
The final product easily simulates over 500 agents at 100+FPS.
With every agent adjusting their behaviour to improve the players perception of a lively city and solving collisions and bottleneck areas at runtime.

To allow the player more control and ways to test the implementation, they are also given control over the layout of the town, buildings can be added or removed, and the behaviour of agents can be modified trough events. Forcing the AI to adapt on the spot.

The AI behaviour is based off the Maslovian hierarchy of needs to find individual goals, while their movement is adjusted to adhere closely to human crowd behaviour using Aggregate Crowd Dynamics, this allows the actors to solve collisions of hundreds of agents on the spot. While continuing on their way to their individual goals.
